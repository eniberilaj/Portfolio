01 / HOW I LEARN

Build it from scratch

I try to implement a method myself before reaching for a library. Writing the stencils, solvers and backward passes by hand, in NumPy and vanilla JavaScript, is the slower path. But it is how I come to understand what a method is really doing, instead of trusting a black box.

02 / HOW I BUILD

One piece at a time

I break large systems into parts I can reason about, like 2D neutronics and 1D heat, then couple them only where it matters using operator splitting and a Picard fixed-point loop. That keeps the code debuggable and the physics legible, which is the whole point of building it.

03 / WHAT I AIM FOR

Make it run live

I aim for results you can interact with in real time, where you move a slider and the physics responds. Holding a 60 FPS budget taught me a lot about vectorisation, profiling, and where the real cost in a simulation actually lives.

Nuclear Reactor Modelling
I wanted to understand how a reactor keeps itself steady, so I built a small browser version. A matrix-free 2D neutron-diffusion solver is coupled to 1D heat flow and 6-group point kinetics, and the core is drawn live in Three.js. It is deliberately teaching-scale, since real cores are fully 3D with far richer cross-section data, but it still captures the feedback loops (Doppler, void, xenon) that make a reactor stable. Move a control rod and watch it respond.

Geometry & Neutronics

Domain & Fuel: 45×45 2D Cartesian mesh modeling a 3411 MWt 4-loop PWR. Features a 3-batch scatter loading pattern (fresh, once-burned, twice-burned) and an albedo reflector boundary (\(\alpha = 0.45\)).

Eigenvalue Solver: Steady-state 2-group neutron diffusion solved via power iteration and matrix-free Jacobi-preconditioned Conjugate Gradient (JCG).
$$ -\nabla \cdot (D_1 \nabla \phi_1) + (\Sigma_{a1} + \Sigma_{s12})\phi_1 = \frac{1}{k}(\nu\Sigma_{f1}\phi_1 + \nu\Sigma_{f2}\phi_2) $$ $$ -\nabla \cdot (D_2 \nabla \phi_2) + \Sigma_{a2}\phi_2 - \Sigma_{s12}\phi_1 = 0 $$

Multiphysics Coupling

Method: Two-way Picard fixed-point iteration coupling the axial 2-group diffusion solver to a 1D enthalpy-rise thermal model.

Feedback Mechanisms:
  • Doppler Broadening: Fast absorption cross-section (\(\Sigma_{a1}\)) scales with the square root of fuel temperature \(\sqrt{T_{fuel}}\).
  • Moderator Voiding: Down-scatter (\(\Sigma_{s12}\)) and fast diffusion (\(D_1\)) degrade as coolant density drops.

Transient Kinetics

Engine: 6-group point kinetics integrated via an implicit-friendly 4th-order Runge-Kutta (RK4) scheme with sub-millisecond step resolution.

Dynamic State: Live scenario macros (SCRAM, LOFA) inject reactivity \(\rho(t)\). The delayed neutron precursor (DNP) concentrations \(C_i(t)\) dictate the system's dynamic response alongside lumped thermal-hydraulic decay heat models.
$$ \frac{dn}{dt} = \left[ \frac{\rho(t) - \beta}{\Lambda} \right] n(t) + \sum_{i=1}^6 \lambda_i C_i(t) $$

Transient Safety Scenarios

These macros execute sub-millisecond Runge-Kutta (RK4) integrations, evaluating the 6-group point kinetics equations coupled with lumped thermal-hydraulic decay heat models.

  • SCRAM (Reactor Trip): Injects massive negative reactivity (\(-15\beta\)). Notice that power does not instantly drop to zero. It hits a decay heat floor dictated by the delayed neutron precursor groups \(C_i(t)\).
  • LOFA (Loss of Flow): Simulates a primary coolant pump failure. As mass flow drops, the coolant temperature spikes, decreasing water density. The negative moderator feedback safely suppresses the fission chain reaction without rods.
  • Asymmetric Rod Drop: Simulates a mechanical failure dropping one RCCA bank. The solver recalculates the spatial mesh, shifting the flux to the unrodded side and severely spiking the localized peaking factor (\(F_q\)).

Time-Delayed Xenon Poisoning

Xenon-135 is the most powerful known neutron poison (\(\sigma_a \approx 2.6 \times 10^6\) barns). Produced indirectly via the decay of Iodine-135, it creates dangerous, non-linear spatial dynamics operating on a scale of hours to days.

  • The Iodine Pit (SCRAM): When the reactor trips, the neutron burnout of Xenon stops completely. However, Iodine-135 continues to decay into Xenon. The core swells with poison for 10-12 hours, creating a "dead time" where restart is physically impossible.
  • Spatial Oscillations: If power is tilted asymmetrically, the high-flux side burns out its Xenon rapidly (in ~3.5 hours), while the low-flux side chokes on Iodine decay. This causes the power peak to slowly swing back and forth across the core over 3 days.
$$ \frac{dXe}{dt} = \gamma_{Xe}\Sigma_f\phi + \lambda_I I - \lambda_{Xe}Xe - \sigma_a^{Xe}Xe\phi $$
Honest notes · what I'd try next
This is a 2D slice on a coarse mesh with a small, fixed two-group cross-section library. That is enough to show real behaviour like criticality, reactivity feedback and the xenon pit, but it is nowhere near a licensing-grade core model. What I'd try next: a full 3D mesh, burnup-dependent cross-sections generated from a lattice code, and proper sub-channel thermal-hydraulics in place of my 1D approximation.

3D Aerodynamics Lab
My take on a wind tunnel. A Python/NumPy engine (app/physics/aero3d.py) solves the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations with a Smagorinsky LES sub-grid model and streams the raw float32 velocity + pressure fields as packed binary; the browser unpacks them into a DataTexture3D, traces RK4 streamlines and reads F1-style loads (Cd · Cl · L/D) from the surface-pressure integral. The grid is coarse and the turbulence model is a simple one, and proper CFD runs millions of cells on a cluster, but building this end to end is how the whole pipeline finally came together for me. Set the geometry and hit Run Aero Solve.

The solver loop (server-side)

A Python + NumPy engine runs the full 3D projection method each timestep, fully vectorised with no Python loops: semi-Lagrangian advection, a Smagorinsky–Lilly LES sub-grid eddy viscosity νt = (CsΔ)²|S|, explicit diffusion, then a pressure-Poisson Jacobi solve to project the field divergence-free. The raw float32 (u,v,w,p) volume is streamed to the browser as packed binary and dropped into a DataTexture3D.

$$ \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} = -(\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} - \frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \nu\nabla^2\mathbf{u},\quad \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}=0 $$

Reading the flow, one layer at a time

CFD literature recommends combining complementary views, so the panel lets you stack them: Pathlines (RK4 streamline tubes) trace the wake and separation; Velocity slices cut planes through the field for a first-pass overview of acceleration and recirculation; the Vector field is a PIV-style arrow rake in the centre plane showing local flow direction and vortex cores; Surface Cp paints the body red→blue at stagnation→suction; Vorticity volume raymarches the shed vortex structures. The F1 loads are physics, not a lookup: Cd, Cl, L/D and the centre of pressure all come from integrating the resolved surface pressure.

F_D = ½ ρ U² A · C_d  |  F_L = ½ ρ U² A · C_l

Honest notes · what I'd try next
The grid is small (~64³) and only runs a handful of timesteps, so the Cd/Cl are the right shape but not validated numbers, and the car is voxelised rather than a true no-slip mesh. What I'd try next: a grid that refines near the surface, a proper wall model instead of stair-stepped boundaries, and validating against a textbook case like the Ahmed body before I trust a single coefficient.

Orbital Debris Simulator · Kinetic Impact & Collision Cascade
I got curious about Kessler syndrome, the idea that a single collision could cascade until debris makes whole orbits unusable. A pure-NumPy engine (app/physics/orbital.py) propagates 20,000+ objects with a vectorised Kepler + J2 solver, plus an atmospheric drag model that decays low orbits until they burn up on re-entry. A spatial-hash collision pass (np.floor + np.lexsort, no loops) then feeds a Kessler cascade that the re-entry sink keeps in balance. Positions stream as raw float32 over a hand-rolled stdlib WebSocket into a Three.js globe. It is far simpler than real conjunction screening, which tracks covariance rather than just distance, but the dynamics behave sensibly. Drag to orbit, scroll to zoom.

The propagator (server-side)

Every object is advanced analytically as pure array algebra, with no Python loop touching the population. Mean motion sets the secular J2 drift of the ascending node Ω̇ and argument of perigee ω̇; Kepler's equation M = E − e·sinE is inverted by a fully vectorised Newton–Raphson sweep, then rotated perifocal→ECI. An exponential atmosphere then bleeds energy from each orbit through its ballistic coefficient, so low passes spiral down and burn up on re-entry.

$$ \begin{aligned} \dot{\Omega} &= -\tfrac{3}{2}\,n\,J_2\!\left(\tfrac{R_E}{p}\right)^{2}\!\cos i \\[2pt] \dot{\omega} &= \tfrac{3}{2}\,n\,J_2\!\left(\tfrac{R_E}{p}\right)^{2}\!\left(2-\tfrac{5}{2}\sin^2 i\right) \end{aligned} $$

Collisions & the Kessler cascade

Each frame the ECI cloud is hashed into voxels with np.floor, ordered by np.lexsort, and same-voxel adjacency yields conjunction candidates with no O(N²) scan. The conjunction rate drives a Kessler model balanced as a source vs. sink: collisions inject fragments while atmospheric re-entry removes them, so the high belt accumulates (watch the altitude profile) while the low shells self-clean, and the Probability of Cascade saturates as a hazard.

$$ P_c = 1 - e^{-\Lambda}, \qquad \Lambda \;\propto\; \sum \text{collisions}\,\cdot\,\frac{N_\text{debris}}{N_0} $$

Honest notes · what I'd try next
I screen conjunctions by gridding distance, not by propagating real covariance ellipsoids the way operators do, and the fragments-per-collision count is a tuned constant rather than the full NASA breakup model. Monte-Carlo Wealth Engine · Cash-Flow & FIRE Projection
I built this to think about my own finances and to learn Monte Carlo methods properly. A pure-NumPy engine (app/finance/engine.py) runs N random market paths over net-of-tax payroll, expenses with loans that roll off, amortised mortgages, rental yield and one-off milestones. From those it reads a financial-independence date (the 4% rule), a safe-withdrawal income, a probability of success and a sensitivity tornado. It assumes returns are independent and normally distributed, whereas real markets have fat tails and sequence-of-returns risk, so treat it as intuition rather than financial advice. Market returns are drawn as independent normal samples, so there are no fat tails, no crashes and no sequence-of-returns risk, which are exactly the things that sink a real plan.
